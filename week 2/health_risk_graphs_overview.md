# Detailed Overview: *Design Features of Graphs in Health Risk Communication: A Systematic Review*

**Article:** Jessica S. Ancker, Yalini Senathirajah, Rita Kukafka, and Justin B. Starren. “Design Features of Graphs in Health Risk Communication: A Systematic Review.” *Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association*, 2006, 13(6), 608-618. DOI: 10.1197/jamia.M2115.

## 1. High-Level Summary

This article is a systematic review of experimental and focus group research on how graphs communicate **quantitative health risks** to patients and members of the public. The authors review studies published after the earlier Lipkus and Hollands review, focusing on how different graphical design features affect comprehension, behavior, preferences, and interpretation of risk.

The central argument is that **graphs are not automatically clearer than numbers or text**. Their effectiveness depends on the communication goal, the viewer’s numeracy and graph literacy, and specific design choices such as whether the graph shows a **part-to-whole relationship**, whether it uses perceptually accurate visual encodings, and how the numbers are formatted.

A major finding is that design features that improve **accurate quantitative reasoning** may differ from features that increase **risk-averse behavior** or from features that viewers simply **prefer**. For example, viewers may like simple or familiar graphics, but those graphics do not always support the most accurate judgments. Similarly, graphics that emphasize only the number of people harmed may increase perceived risk and risk-avoidant behavior, but they may also distort understanding if the denominator is not visually clear.

## 2. Purpose and Scope of the Review

The review examines how graphics are used to communicate health-related probabilities, frequencies, and chances. The authors are especially interested in patient education, shared decision-making, informed consent, risk appraisal, and counseling about difficult choices such as cancer screening or genetic testing.

The authors frame health risk communication as a difficult design problem because many people struggle with numerical probabilities. Small probabilities are especially challenging: people may overestimate them, underestimate them, or treat them as effectively zero. These difficulties can be worsened by limited numeracy, low health literacy, stress, aging, or discomfort with numbers.

Graphs are attractive because they can make risk information more visually engaging and may reduce the need for mental arithmetic. However, graph interpretation still requires learned skills, and patients often interpret graphics differently than designers intend.

## 3. Search Methods

The authors searched for evaluation studies of graphs that communicated probabilities, frequencies, or chances of health outcomes and that were not already covered in Lipkus and Hollands’ 1999 review.

### Sources searched

The authors searched:

- PsycInfo
- MEDLINE
- CINAHL
- ACM Portal
- Websites of selected journals, including *Health Psychology*, *Risk Analysis*, and *Medical Decision Making*
- Reference lists and citation tools

### Time range

The review focused on studies from **1998 through 2005**.

### Inclusion focus

The review included experimental and focus group studies about graphs of quantitative health risks.

### Exclusions

The authors excluded:

- Commentaries and instructional articles
- Studies of pain scales or utility measures
- Studies of illustrations communicating threat or causality rather than quantitative risk
- Studies where graphics were not used as an independent variable
- Dissertations

### Final corpus

The review discusses **24 studies**.

## 4. Key Problem: Different Evaluation Goals Produce Different “Best” Graphs

The authors emphasize that graphics studies use different outcome measures, and the “best” graph depends on the purpose of the risk communication.

### 4.1 Improving understanding

Some studies evaluate whether viewers understand quantitative information accurately. Example tasks include:

- Estimating a proportion from a graph
- Comparing two risks
- Reading survival values from a curve
- Calculating differences between survival values at two points in time

From this perspective, graphics are judged by whether they support accurate reasoning. Distorted axes, misleading framing, and graphics that exaggerate differences may be criticized as unethical because they push perceptions away from the actual probability.

### 4.2 Changing behavior or behavioral intentions

Other studies evaluate whether a graphic changes behavior, risk avoidance, or willingness to act. For example, some studies measured whether participants would pay more for a safer product after seeing a risk graphic.

From this perspective, a graph may be considered effective if it motivates a desired health behavior, even if it uses framing or salience effects.

### 4.3 Understanding preferences, acceptance, and trust

Many studies evaluate what users like or dislike, because real-world communication can fail if users reject or ignore the graphic. Outcomes include:

- Perceived ease of use
- Satisfaction with information
- Anxiety
- Perceived persuasiveness
- Preference among graphic formats

A core finding is that **user preference does not reliably predict comprehension**. Participants sometimes preferred graphs that produced worse quantitative judgments.

## 5. Three Design Features the Review Uses to Interpret the Literature

The authors organize their review around three recurring design features.

### 5.1 Part-to-whole relationships

A graph communicates a part-to-whole relationship when the viewer can visually compare the number of affected people with the total population at risk. For example, a stacked bar chart from 0% to 100% with a colored segment for the affected group makes the denominator visually explicit.

This feature matters because part-to-whole graphs can help viewers attend to the actual proportion rather than only the numerator. When graphs show only the number harmed and omit the denominator visually, viewers may perceive the risk as larger and become more risk-averse.

### 5.2 Basic graphical perception abilities

The review draws on Cleveland and McGill’s work on graphical perception. Some visual judgments are more accurate than others:

| Visual judgment task | General accuracy |
|---|---:|
| Position or length along a common scale | High |
| Angle or slope | Good |
| Area | Fair |
| Volume, color, or grayscale density | Poor |

This hierarchy helps explain why some graphs are easier to interpret than others. Bar charts can be effective because people judge length against a common baseline accurately. Pie charts and area-based displays may be harder because they require less precise visual judgments.

### 5.3 Numerical format

Different formats for the same probability can produce different reasoning outcomes. For example, 6%, 0.06, and 6 in 100 are mathematically equivalent but not psychologically equivalent.

The authors note that ratios are harder to compare when they use different denominators. For example, comparing “1 in 250” with “1 in 1000” is harder than comparing “4 in 1000” with “1 in 1000.” Ratios with the same denominator, often called natural frequencies, tend to support better reasoning.

## 6. Major Graph Types Reviewed

## 6.1 Icon Arrays

Icon arrays show risk as a group of individual icons, such as dots, human figures, or squares. Each icon represents one person or unit. Icon arrays often support discrete-frequency reasoning, which can be easier for many viewers than abstract percentages.

### Key findings

Icon arrays can reduce the influence of vivid anecdotes. In one study, participants imagined choosing between bypass surgery and balloon angioplasty. Anecdotes about treatment success or failure strongly influenced choices. When participants also saw icon arrays showing the actual success rates, the anecdote effect became smaller.

The review distinguishes between icon arrays that make part-to-whole relationships clear and those that do not. Sequential icon arrays, where affected and unaffected icons are grouped in an orderly block, make proportions easier to judge. Random icon arrays may better convey chance or randomness, but they make proportions harder to estimate.

### Figure 1: Part-to-whole icon array with sequential arrangement

![Figure 1: Part-to-whole icon array with sequential arrangement](health_risk_graphs_overview_assets/figure_from_page_3_1.jpeg)

This figure compares success rates for balloon angioplasty and bypass surgery using icon arrays. The affected and unaffected groups are visually separated, making the part-to-whole relationship available. Because the icons are arranged in blocks and touch each other, viewers may process the display partly as an area judgment rather than by counting individual icons.

### Design implication

Sequential icon arrays are useful when the goal is to help viewers estimate or compare proportions accurately. Random icon arrays may be useful when the goal is to emphasize chance, but they can reduce accuracy when viewers must estimate proportions.

## 6.2 Part-to-Whole Relationships in Icon Arrays and Bar Graphs

The review discusses a series of studies by Stone and colleagues showing that graphs can increase risk-averse behavior when they emphasize only the numerator of a risk ratio.

For example, when participants compared fictitious products with different probabilities of harm, they were more willing to pay for the safer product when the number harmed was displayed graphically as icons, asterisks, faces, or bars. However, when the graph showed the full denominator visually, the increase in risk aversion disappeared.

The authors call this a **foreground effect**: when a graph foregrounds only the harmed individuals, it increases the salience of the harm. When the whole at-risk population is visible, the viewer sees the risk in proportion.

### Figure 2: Icon array without part-to-whole information

![Figure 2: Icon array without part-to-whole information](health_risk_graphs_overview_assets/figure_from_page_4_1.jpeg)

This figure shows only the number of people injured, not the full at-risk population. Because the denominator is not visually shown, the difference between the two risks is emphasized. This type of display can increase perceived risk and risk-averse behavior.

### Figure 3: Part-to-whole bar graph

![Figure 3: Part-to-whole bar graph](health_risk_graphs_overview_assets/figure_from_page_4_2.jpeg)

This stacked bar chart shows affected and unaffected people within the full population at risk. The denominator is visually present, so the viewer can judge the affected portion relative to the whole. This design reduced the salience-driven risk-avoidance effect seen in numerator-only graphics.

### Design implication

When the goal is accurate quantitative judgment, designers should show both numerator and denominator. When the goal is behavior change, numerator-focused graphics may be more persuasive, but they risk exaggerating perceived risk.

## 6.3 Human Figures Versus Other Icons

The review finds mixed evidence about whether human-shaped icons are better than abstract symbols.

Some qualitative studies found that women considered human figure icons more meaningful, easier to identify with, and easier to understand than bar charts. However, experimental work found no meaningful behavioral difference between displays using human faces and displays using asterisks.

### Design implication

Human figures may improve emotional identification or perceived meaningfulness, but evidence does not clearly show that they improve quantitative understanding or decision quality. Designers should not assume human icons are always superior.

## 6.4 Random Arrangement in Icon Arrays

Random icon arrays scatter affected individuals throughout the larger group rather than grouping them sequentially.

### Figure 4: Random-arrangement icon array

![Figure 4: Random-arrangement icon array](health_risk_graphs_overview_assets/figure_from_page_5_1.jpeg)

This figure illustrates a random arrangement in which affected individuals are scattered across the icon field. The design may communicate randomness or chance, but it makes the proportion harder to judge because viewers cannot rely on simple length or area comparisons.

### Key findings

Viewers were less accurate at estimating proportions in random arrays than in sequentially arranged arrays. Some focus group participants disliked random arrays because they felt they had to count the icons. However, some participants said the random arrangement better conveyed the idea of chance.

### Design implication

Use random arrangements cautiously. They may be helpful for communicating randomness, but they are weaker when the task requires precise proportion estimation.

## 6.5 Risk Tables, Ladders, and Scales

Risk ladders and scales place a particular risk within a broader range of risks. They can show very low to very high risks and may include familiar comparison risks to provide context.

### Key findings

Risk ladders and scales can help viewers place unfamiliar risks in context. However, perceptions are heavily influenced by the graph’s layout. If a risk is placed visually closer to the “high risk” end of a scale, viewers may perceive it as more serious even when the numerical value has not changed.

The review also discusses logarithmic risk scales, such as the Paling Perspective Scale, which can display a very wide range of risk magnitudes. However, the authors note that it is unclear how viewers interpret logarithmic scales.

![Figure 5: Risk scale with comparative risks](health_risk_graphs_overview_assets/figure_from_page_6_1.jpeg)

This figure places blood transfusion risks alongside other risks such as dying in a car accident or from smoking. It uses a broad risk scale to help viewers contextualize rare risks. The review notes that the interpretability of the logarithmic scale is uncertain.

### Figure 6: Magnifier risk scale

![Figure 6: Magnifier risk scale](health_risk_graphs_overview_assets/figure_from_page_6_2.jpeg)

This scale includes a magnifying lens at the low end to help users report very small probabilities. One study found that the magnifier helped users make appropriately low estimates for rare events, but another study found that it also lowered estimates for more common events.

### Design implication

Risk ladders and scales can provide useful context, but designers must be careful with scale construction, comparison risks, and visual placement because these choices can strongly alter perceived risk.

## 6.6 Survival and Mortality Curves

Survival and mortality curves show changes in risk over time. They are cognitively complex because viewers must interpret trends, slopes, time spans, and sometimes differences between curves.

### Key findings

Survival curves can help viewers attend to long-term outcomes that may be ignored in numeric summaries. However, many lay viewers need instruction to interpret them correctly. In one study, only 74% of a jury-pool sample could determine the number of survivors at a specific time point from a survival curve, and only 55% could calculate the difference in survival between two time points.

The review also highlights framing effects. When the same treatment information is framed as mortality rather than survival, viewers may make different choices. Mortality framing can make short-term death risk more salient.

Another important finding is that viewers are influenced by the visual distance between curves, not just the numerical difference. Changing the time scale can flatten or stretch curves, altering perceived treatment effectiveness.

### Figure 7: Survival curves

![Figure 7: Survival curves](health_risk_graphs_overview_assets/figure_from_page_7_1.jpeg)

This figure shows how survival curves can visually change depending on the time span displayed. The top chart shows 15 years of survival data; the bottom chart shows only the first 5 years stretched to the same width. The visual distance between curves changes, which can affect viewers’ estimates of treatment effectiveness.

### Design implication

Survival curves can be powerful but require careful design and often require instruction. Designers should avoid axis choices that visually exaggerate or minimize treatment differences.

## 7. Persuasiveness and Comprehension

The review discusses evidence that text can sometimes outperform graphics. In one study, text descriptions of statistical relationships between disease and genetics were better understood and perceived as stronger evidence than bar charts showing the same data.

The authors caution that this finding may partly reflect differences in how the information was described. The graph used the term “relative mortality rate,” while the text used a more direct phrase such as “about 20% more likely to die early deaths.” Even so, the study supports the broader point that graphics do not automatically improve comprehension.

### Design implication

Graphs should be paired with clear explanatory text, especially when the concept is unfamiliar or statistically complex.

## 8. Data Scale: Discrete, Ordinal, and Continuous Formats

The review notes that people may give different risk estimates depending on whether the risk is elicited with:

- Discrete formats, such as icons or “out of 100” displays
- Ordinal formats, such as low/medium/high
- Continuous formats, such as a 0%-100% scale

For example, women estimating breast cancer risk produced different estimates when using icon displays versus continuous scales. Icons elicited estimates that were higher and farther from epidemiological estimates.

Word-based ordinal scales sometimes performed better for eliciting subjective perceptions because they were reliable and usable. However, they are less precise and may not support accurate quantitative interpretation.

### Design implication

The best format for **showing risk information** may differ from the best format for **eliciting a patient’s perceived risk**.

## 9. Multiple Quantitative Endpoints and Format Comparisons

The review summarizes studies comparing several visual formats in the same task.

### Feldman-Stewart et al.

This work compared several formats, including:

- Vertical part-to-whole bar charts
- Horizontal part-to-whole bar charts
- Number pairs
- Random icon arrays
- Sequential icon arrays
- Pie charts

Participants were slowest and least accurate with pie charts and random icon arrays. Estimates of differences between quantities were best with number pairs and sequentially arranged icons. Importantly, participants did no better with the formats they preferred.

### Elting et al.

In a study with physicians, participants reviewed fictitious clinical trial data and decided whether a trial should be stopped. Most noticed the high failure rate when shown icon arrays or numerical deals, but fewer noticed it with pie charts or stacked bar charts. However, physicians tended to like the bar graphs and dislike the icon arrays, again showing that preference does not necessarily align with performance.

### Design implication

Designers should evaluate graphs with task-based measures, not only preference ratings.

## 10. Main Conclusions From the Review

## 10.1 The “best” graph depends on the communication goal

A graph that improves quantitative accuracy may not be the graph that best motivates behavior change. Likewise, the graph people prefer may not be the graph they understand best.

## 10.2 Part-to-whole information is crucial for accurate risk judgment

Graphs that show both numerator and denominator help viewers understand proportions. Graphs that show only the numerator can inflate perceived risk and promote risk-averse behavior.

## 10.3 Sequential icon arrays can support proportion judgments

Sequentially arranged icon arrays are generally better than random arrays when users need to estimate or compare proportions. Random arrays may convey chance but are harder to read quantitatively.

## 10.4 Familiarity and visual simplicity are not enough

People often prefer familiar or visually simple graphics, but these preferences may not correspond to accurate interpretation. A simpler graph can omit important context or encourage misleading judgments.

## 10.5 Instruction and text explanations often matter

Many graphics, including survival curves and complex risk displays, require explanation. Patients may need text, labels, examples, or training to interpret them correctly.

## 10.6 Health risk communication should account for numeracy, literacy, education, and culture

The authors emphasize that graph literacy varies. Educational level, numeracy, health literacy, and cultural context can all shape how people interpret risk graphics and whether they trust them.

## 11. Practical Design Recommendations

| Design issue | Recommendation | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Communicating absolute risk | Show the whole population at risk, not only affected cases | Prevents numerator-only exaggeration |
| Comparing proportions | Use part-to-whole bars or sequential icon arrays | Supports easier proportion judgment |
| Communicating randomness | Consider random icon arrays, but use with caution | May convey chance but reduces quantitative accuracy |
| Communicating very small risks | Provide context and clear numeric labels | Small probabilities are often misread |
| Using risk ladders/scales | Choose comparison risks and scale endpoints carefully | Visual placement can distort perceived magnitude |
| Showing survival over time | Use clear axes and provide instruction | Survival curves are cognitively demanding |
| Evaluating designs | Measure comprehension and behavior, not just preference | Liked formats may perform poorly |
| Supporting low numeracy users | Use common denominators, simple frequencies, and explanatory text | Reduces mental calculation burden |

## 12. Relevance to Uncertainty Visualization and Everyday Decision-Making

Although this article focuses on health risk communication, it is highly relevant to broader uncertainty visualization work. The review shows that representing probabilities visually can help, but only when the visual form aligns with the viewer’s task and perceptual abilities.

For uncertainty displays in domains such as transit, weather, medical decisions, or predictive systems, this review supports several principles:

- Show uncertainty in a way that preserves meaningful denominators or possible outcomes.
- Avoid visual encodings that require difficult perceptual judgments when simpler encodings are possible.
- Do not assume that users understand a graphic without instruction.
- Distinguish between helping users make accurate judgments and persuading them toward a behavior.
- Test displays with realistic tasks, because user preference alone is not enough.

## 14. Bottom-Line Takeaway

The article’s core message is that risk graphics are powerful but not automatically intuitive. Effective health risk communication requires matching the graph design to the communication goal, making denominators and part-to-whole relationships clear, using perceptually accurate encodings, and accounting for user differences in numeracy and graph literacy. Designers should evaluate whether a graph actually improves understanding or decisions rather than relying on whether viewers like it.
