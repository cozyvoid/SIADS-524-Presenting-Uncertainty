# **The (Non)Use of Generative AI In This Course**

Data science, like most other fields, evolves over time. New ideas, strategies, and technologies continue to push data science in new directions. The use of generative AI and other forms of artificial intelligence is no different. Certainly, these technologies have the ability to transform the way we think and work in data science.

Generative AI refers to artificial intelligence systems that generate new content—such as text, images, music, or code—based on patterns learned from data. These systems typically use models like deep learning neural networks (e.g., transformers, GANs, VAEs) to produce novel outputs rather than just selecting from predefined responses. 

A system qualifies as generative AI if it creates or synthesizes new content based on learned patterns rather than simply retrieving or modifying existing content. Examples include:

* Text generation (e.g., ChatGPT, Bard, Claude)  
* Image generation (e.g., DALL\*E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion)  
* Music and video generation (e.g., Jukebox, Runway)  
* Code generation (e.g., GitHub Copilot, ChatGPT)

Other tools, like spell/grammar checkers and search engines, are not classified as generative AI and are not included in this discussion.

The question we face is how to use generative AI productively in our work. Our approach may look different depending on the context, project, organization, and situation. In the case of this class, our goal and objective is to **learn** data science principles, techniques, and (most importantly) applications. The question remains, "in what way(s) can generative AI enhance our learning journey?"

Though a relatively new application space, generative AI can be helpful as a tutor to refine conceptual understanding, to provide illustrations, and to summarize content. Of course, like any source of information, we should be careful not to take everything it provides at face value. When it comes to generative AI, we should "trust but verify" its output.

There are, however, not all uses of generative AI enhance the learning journey. As a rule, whenever the algorithms are carrying the cognitive load for you, it is probably counter productive to your learning. Put differently, if the algorithm is doing the work for you, it is unlikely that you are learning deeply.

Given this reality, we state in the syllabus that any and all use of generative AI on assignments, quizzes, or other graded items is **prohibited** in this course. This includes:

* assistance in writing code for assignments.  
* assistance in generating solutions for assignments  
* assistance in answering short answers  
* assistance in answering quiz items  
* assistance in generating text for answers (not including spell-check or grammar-check)

It’s not that your instructors are old-fashioned, out-of-date, or otherwise contrarian. We’re not. Rather, we know what it takes to succeed as a data scientist, to navigate and think through problems, and to excel in job interviews. All these require actual knowledge that is thoroughly internalized, and relying on AI to do the thinking for you (cognitive offloading) is counter productive to that. As cognitive scientist Carl Hendricks argues:

Recent research is beginning to quantify what teachers have long suspected: when AI does the thinking, students stop doing it themselves. In a [2025 mixed-methods study published in](https://nam10.safelinks.protection.outlook.com/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack.com%2Fredirect%2Feca43609-bd05-4f53-b36b-ecfbe2776b3a%3Fj%3DeyJ1Ijoid3p5aTUifQ.4HopGbfHHoL1vt0L1xU-C0p8-WenHKytJa3nNrzsT-4&data=05%7C02%7Cnsheltrown%40nhaschools.com%7C220c556413d44fc63b9d08de1ea04b99%7C2a47946cc6024a92a03ff7392186a1d8%7C0%7C0%7C638981871280234598%7CUnknown%7CTWFpbGZsb3d8eyJFbXB0eU1hcGkiOnRydWUsIlYiOiIwLjAuMDAwMCIsIlAiOiJXaW4zMiIsIkFOIjoiTWFpbCIsIldUIjoyfQ%3D%3D%7C0%7C%7C%7C&sdata=eVcd9eB8%2FtV7R7ETUjNUJyoc5U8Abp9MJVwzFbksYmc%3D&reserved=0) [*Societies*](https://nam10.safelinks.protection.outlook.com/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack.com%2Fredirect%2Feca43609-bd05-4f53-b36b-ecfbe2776b3a%3Fj%3DeyJ1Ijoid3p5aTUifQ.4HopGbfHHoL1vt0L1xU-C0p8-WenHKytJa3nNrzsT-4&data=05%7C02%7Cnsheltrown%40nhaschools.com%7C220c556413d44fc63b9d08de1ea04b99%7C2a47946cc6024a92a03ff7392186a1d8%7C0%7C0%7C638981871280249937%7CUnknown%7CTWFpbGZsb3d8eyJFbXB0eU1hcGkiOnRydWUsIlYiOiIwLjAuMDAwMCIsIlAiOiJXaW4zMiIsIkFOIjoiTWFpbCIsIldUIjoyfQ%3D%3D%7C0%7C%7C%7C&sdata=uv0c0PFsGT1PqTmglo523wUXVwn2Dt6DjuGegLzRLec%3D&reserved=0), Michael Gerlich found that frequent AI tool use was strongly negatively correlated with critical thinking ability, largely because of a mechanism known as *cognitive offloading*. The more participants relied on AI to remember, decide, or explain, the less capable they became of reasoning independently. Younger users (those most immersed in generative tools) showed the greatest decline. Gerlich describes this as a kind of “cognitive dependence”: efficiency rising as understanding falls. It’s precisely this frictionless fluency that creates the illusion of learning; the sense that one is mastering material when, in fact, the machine is doing the mastery. (Hendricks, 2025\)

It’s not that we think generative AI cannot enhance your learning in this course. We know it can\!. The **allowable** uses of generative AI in this course include:

* developing  a better understanding of a course concept  
* summarizing course materials  
* extending course concepts into new areas unaddressed by the course

In short, you cannot use generative AI to help you do the work of the course (assignments, assessments). You can use generative AI to assist you in the learning of the course.

If you have any questions regarding what is or is not allowed in this course in terms of use of generative AI, please reach out to the instructional team.
