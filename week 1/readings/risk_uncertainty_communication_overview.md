# Detailed Overview: *Risk and Uncertainty Communication* by David Spiegelhalter

**Article:** Spiegelhalter, D. (2017). *Risk and Uncertainty Communication*. *Annual Review of Statistics and Its Application*, 4, 31-60.  
**Core topic:** How statistical risks, probabilities, and uncertainties can be communicated to nonspecialist audiences in ways that are accurate, understandable, and appropriately cautious.

---

## 1. Executive Summary

David Spiegelhalter reviews how risks and uncertainties arising from statistical analysis are communicated across many domains, including individual health decisions, pharmaceuticals, weather, natural hazards, climate change, environmental exposures, intelligence, industrial reliability, national risks, and global catastrophic risks.

The article's central message is that risk communication is not simply a matter of presenting correct numbers. Effective communication requires clarity about the communication objective, a careful understanding of the audience, appropriate numerical and visual formats, acknowledgment of uncertainty, and testing of communication materials with the intended users.

A recurring theme is that audiences do not interpret risk information in a purely mathematical way. Their understanding is shaped by framing, numeracy, trust, prior beliefs, cultural values, emotional responses, and the perceived fairness or dread associated with a hazard. Therefore, the same numerical risk can lead to very different perceptions depending on how it is expressed.

The article is especially useful because it distinguishes three linked but different communication problems:

1. **Communicating risk** - explaining the probability and consequences of possible outcomes.
2. **Communicating uncertainty** - explaining limits in the numerical estimates.
3. **Communicating confidence** - explaining how much trust should be placed in the analytic process itself.

---

## 2. Purpose and Scope of the Article

Spiegelhalter focuses on the communication of statistical risk assessments to nonspecialist audiences. He deliberately avoids a full discussion of risk governance, regulation, risk management, or what counts as an “acceptable” risk. The emphasis is on communication: how risk information is represented through numbers, words, graphics, uncertainty statements, and case-specific practices.

The article begins with individual health risks because this area has been heavily researched. It then moves into broader case studies where risks affect communities, nations, or the globe.

The article does **not** attempt to produce universal rules. Instead, it develops practical guidance and repeatedly emphasizes that risk communication must be adapted to the audience, context, and objective.

## 3. Foundational Concepts

### 3.1 Risk

In everyday language, “risk” usually refers to something bad that might happen. The article contrasts this with more technical definitions, such as the ISO 31000 definition of risk as the “effect of uncertainty on objectives.”

A key distinction is between **hazard** and **risk**:

| Concept | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Hazard | A potential source of harm | Flying six miles above the ground in a machine is inherently hazardous. |
| Risk | The actual chance and consequence of harm after safeguards or mitigating actions | Commercial flying is extremely safe because engineering, regulation, training, and monitoring reduce the actual risk. |

In safety and disaster contexts, risk is often represented as:

> **Risk = hazard x vulnerability x exposure**

This means that harm depends not only on the existence of a hazard, but also on who or what is exposed and how vulnerable they are.

### 3.2 Rational Decision-Making Under Risk

The article summarizes a classical decision-theoretic approach to risk:

1. List possible actions and consequences.
2. Assign values to possible outcomes.
3. Assign probabilities to consequences under each action.
4. Choose the action that maximizes expected benefit.

This model works best in “small world” situations where probabilities and outcomes are well-defined, such as roulette or other controlled gambling examples. Real-world risk communication is harder because the “big world” is messy: probabilities may be uncertain, evidence may be incomplete, values may be contested, and models may fail to capture important realities.

### 3.3 Uncertainty

Spiegelhalter distinguishes three broad types of uncertainty:

| Type of uncertainty | Meaning | How it is usually communicated |
|---|---|---|
| **Aleatory uncertainty** | Inherent randomness or chance variation | Probabilities, event rates, risk estimates |
| **Epistemic uncertainty** | Uncertainty about model structure, parameters, data, or assumptions | Confidence intervals, ranges, sensitivity analyses, Bayesian distributions |
| **Ontological uncertainty** | Uncertainty about whether the model itself adequately represents reality | Qualitative caveats, limitations, humility about unknown unknowns |

This distinction is important because not all uncertainty can be reduced to a single probability. Some uncertainty can be quantified, but some reflects deeper limits in scientific understanding or modeling.

### 3.4 Good Communication

A communication is successful only in relation to its purpose. Possible goals include improving comprehension, supporting informed decisions, reducing misunderstanding, changing behavior, or building resistance to misleading anecdotes.

Spiegelhalter makes an important ethical distinction between:

- **Persuasion**, where the communicator seeks to move people toward a specific behavior or attitude.
- **Informing**, where the communicator seeks to help people understand evidence and make their own decisions.

The article mainly assumes the second goal: fulfilling a duty to inform.

## 4. Psychology and Sociology of Risk Perception

### 4.1 Why Numbers Alone Are Not Enough

Early approaches to risk communication often assumed that experts simply needed to calculate risks correctly and explain them clearly. This is sometimes called the **deficit model**, because it treats public disagreement as a knowledge deficit.

Spiegelhalter argues that this model is inadequate. People’s responses to risk are not merely caused by ignorance. They are shaped by emotion, trust, values, fairness, social identity, and prior experience.

### 4.2 “Fear Factors” That Increase Perceived Risk

The article summarizes psychological research showing that hazards tend to be perceived as riskier when they are:

- Uncontrollable
- Catastrophic
- Fatal or dreadful
- Inequitably distributed
- Poorly understood
- Novel
- Delayed in their harmful effects

For example, radiation from a nuclear accident triggers many of these factors: it is invisible, feared, poorly understood by many people, associated with catastrophic harm, and often perceived as imposed rather than voluntarily chosen.

### 4.3 Outrage, Trust, and Blame

When crises occur, people respond not only to the hazard itself but also to outrage, anger, and blame. A public response can therefore be intensified if people believe the hazard was caused by negligence, secrecy, unfairness, or institutional failure.

This leads to a key lesson: trust is not built by simply telling people to trust experts. Institutions need to demonstrate **trustworthiness** through transparency, listening, competence, honesty, and genuine engagement.

### 4.4 Affect and Cultural Cognition

The article also discusses the **affect heuristic**, where people’s overall positive or negative feelings toward an activity influence how they judge its risks and benefits. For example, if someone has a strong negative feeling toward nuclear technology, they may judge its risks as high and its benefits as low, regardless of technical analysis.

The article also discusses cultural cognition: people’s views about risk can align with broader worldviews, identities, and political or cultural commitments. This helps explain why more information does not always produce convergence of opinion.

## 5. Communicating Probabilities of Events

The article’s most practical section focuses on how to communicate probabilities, especially individual health risks.

### 5.1 Positive and Negative Framing

The same risk can be framed positively or negatively:

- Negative frame: “10% of patients experience a bad side effect.”
- Positive frame: “90% of patients do not experience a bad side effect.”

These are mathematically equivalent, but they can feel different. Positive framing tends to make risks seem smaller and may increase willingness to accept treatment.

Spiegelhalter recommends avoiding one-sided framing when possible. A balanced presentation should include both the chance of the event and the chance of the event not occurring.

### 5.2 Percentages vs Frequencies

A probability can be expressed as a percentage or as a frequency:

| Format | Example |
|---|---|
| Percentage | 20% |
| Frequency | 20 out of 100 people like you |

The article does not claim one format is always superior. Frequencies can be especially useful because they make the denominator visible and work well with icon arrays. Percentages can be clearer in contexts like weather forecasts or unique events.

The more important issue is making the **reference class** clear.

### 5.3 Reference Class

The reference class is the group or set of cases to which the probability applies.

For example, “30% chance of rain” should mean something like:

> In situations like this, rain occurs about 30% of the time.

It does **not** mean that it will rain for 30% of the day or over 30% of the area, although many people interpret it that way.

A good risk statement specifies:

- Who the risk applies to
- Over what time period
- What outcome is being counted
- What comparison group is being used

### 5.4 Absolute Risk vs Relative Risk

The article strongly emphasizes the difference between absolute and relative risk.

The article’s processed meat example shows why this matters. A daily bacon sandwich was reported as increasing bowel cancer risk by 18%. This sounds large because it is a **relative increase**. In absolute terms, the article reframes the risk as roughly increasing lifetime bowel cancer cases from 6 out of 100 people to 7 out of 100 people.

| Measure | Non-bacon eaters | Daily bacon eaters | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Event rate | 6% | 7% | Absolute risk changes by about 1 percentage point. |
| Expected frequency | 6 out of 100 | 7 out of 100 | One extra case per 100 lifetime daily bacon eaters. |
| Relative risk | — | 18% increase | Sounds larger because it compares proportional change. |

The practical lesson is that communicators should usually foreground **absolute risk**, especially in health contexts. Relative risk may still be useful in low-probability catastrophic contexts, where a small absolute risk may still be meaningfully elevated.

### 5.5 “1 in X” Risks and Ratio Bias

The article warns against overusing “1 in X” formats because people often struggle to compare them. For example, many people do not immediately identify whether 1 in 10, 1 in 100, or 1 in 1,000 is the largest risk.

Frequency comparisons are clearer when the denominator is kept fixed:

- Clearer: 6 out of 100 vs 7 out of 100
- Less clear: 1 in 16 vs 1 in 14

### 5.6 Time Interval

Risk statements should specify the time interval. “10% chance” is incomplete unless the audience knows whether the risk is per day, per year, over ten years, or over a lifetime.

Better examples:

- “10 out of 100 people each year”
- “12 out of 100 people like you will have a heart attack or stroke within the next 10 years”

## 6. Visual Communication of Risk

Graphics can help people grasp the gist of risk information, especially when designed carefully. However, visuals are not automatically better than numbers. They must be tested and matched to the audience and purpose.

### 6.1 23andMe Individual Risk Display

The article includes a 23andMe example showing the author’s estimated psoriasis risk compared with an average risk. The display uses icon arrays and frequencies to compare 3.9 out of 100 with 1.8 out of 100.

![Figure 1: 23andMe risk display showing individual psoriasis risk compared with average risk.](risk_uncertainty_overview_assets/figure_1_23andme_risk.png)

**Key communication lesson:** The display combines a frequency format with a visual part-to-whole comparison. This helps audiences see both the affected and unaffected portions of the reference group.

### 6.2 Icon Arrays and Incremental Risk

The article uses an icon array for the bacon sandwich example. It shows 6 bowel cancer cases out of 100 in the baseline group and one additional case in the daily bacon group.

![Figure 2: Icon array showing incremental bowel cancer risk associated with daily processed meat consumption.](risk_uncertainty_overview_assets/figure_2_icon_array_incremental_risk.png)

**Key communication lesson:** Incremental risk formats can make small absolute changes clearer. However, scattered icons can also make counting and comparison harder, especially for low-numeracy audiences. Blocking icons into organized groups may be better when precise comparison is needed.

### 6.3 General Lessons About Graphics

Spiegelhalter summarizes several lessons from research on risk graphics:

- Graphs can communicate the overall gist quickly.
- Tables may be clearer for precise numerical details.
- Line graphs are useful for trends.
- Bar charts are useful for comparisons.
- Pie charts are often poor for comparing risks.
- Icon arrays can help low-numeracy audiences, but only if designed simply.
- Overly complex visuals, animations, and interactivity can backfire.
- Good labels and clear denominators are essential.

The article’s “less can be more” point is important: simplified graphics often support better understanding than visually impressive but cognitively demanding designs.

## 7. Using Words to Communicate Risk

The article warns against using words alone to communicate numerical risks. Words like “common,” “rare,” “likely,” or “high risk” are interpreted differently by different people.

### 7.1 Drug Side Effect Labels

The European Medicines Agency uses frequency bands for adverse drug events:

| Verbal description | Frequency group |
|---|---:|
| Very common | >= 1/10 |
| Common | >= 1/100 to < 1/10 |
| Uncommon | >= 1/1,000 to < 1/100 |
| Rare | >= 1/10,000 to < 1/1,000 |
| Very rare | < 1/10,000 |
| Frequency not known | Cannot be estimated from available data |

A major issue is that patients may interpret these words very differently from regulators. For example, “common” may be interpreted as much more frequent than intended.

### 7.2 Recommended Approach

Words can be useful, but they should usually be paired with numbers:

> “Common: may affect up to 1 in 10 people.”

This gives the word emotional and practical meaning while anchoring it to a numerical range.

## 8. Communicating Chronic and Long-Term Risks

Some risks unfold over years or decades. Spiegelhalter reviews several ways to communicate chronic risks.

### 8.1 Common Metrics

| Metric | Example | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hazard ratio | Risk is 1.5 times higher | Useful in epidemiology | Can exaggerate perceived effect if baseline risk is hidden |
| Absolute risk at a fixed time | 12% risk over 10 years | Clearer for decisions | Requires clear reference class and time interval |
| Lifetime risk | 1 in 2 people born after 1960 will be diagnosed with cancer | Attention-grabbing | Can feel fatalistic or lack context |
| Survival curves | Probability of survival over time | Shows time dynamics | Can be difficult without explanation |
| Life expectancy change | Smoking reduces life expectancy | Intuitive metaphor | Average effect may not feel personally relevant |
| Effective age | “Heart age” | Personally salient | Can oversimplify complex risk |
| Time lost per exposure | “Each cigarette takes minutes off life” | Vivid | Metaphorical, not literal |
| Comparators | Air pollution expressed in cigarette equivalents | Makes abstract risks concrete | Can trigger inappropriate emotional reactions |

### 8.2 Comparators and Small Risks

For very small risks, people struggle to distinguish probabilities such as 1 in 10,000 and 1 in 100,000. Risk ladders and comparators can help, but comparators must be chosen carefully.

The article discusses the **micromort**, defined as a 1 in 1,000,000 chance of death. This allows very different hazards to be compared on a common scale, such as surgery, hang-gliding, drug use, or military service.

## 9. Communicating Uncertainty About Numerical Risks

The article distinguishes uncertainty about the risk number from uncertainty about the whole analytic process.

### 9.1 Levels of Precision

A communicator can express uncertainty through:

1. **Rounding** - e.g., saying 10% rather than 10.2% to avoid false precision.
2. **Ranges or intervals** - e.g., confidence intervals.
3. **Statistical significance displays** - e.g., funnel plots or standard-error boundaries.
4. **Verbal qualifiers** - e.g., “best estimate from available evidence.”
5. **Refusal to quantify** - when evidence is not strong enough.

### 9.2 Funnel Plot Example

The article includes a funnel plot of surgical mortality in UK pediatric heart surgery centers. The plot shows mortality percentage against number of cases, with 95% and 99.8% limits.

![Figure 3: Funnel plot showing pediatric heart surgery mortality against number of cases.](risk_uncertainty_overview_assets/figure_3_funnel_plot.png)

**Key communication lesson:** Funnel plots can show both estimated risks and whether outcomes are statistically unusual given sample size. They also reveal patterns such as volume effects. However, they require explanation, and viewers may overfocus on whether points are above or below an average line.

## 10. Communicating Confidence in the Analytic Process

Some uncertainty goes beyond numerical intervals. Analysts may be unsure whether the model, data, assumptions, or evidence base adequately represent reality.

The article identifies several ways to communicate confidence:

| Approach | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Explicit model uncertainty | Use alternative models or Bayesian model averaging. |
| Strength-of-evidence scales | Rate evidence as high, moderate, low, or very low. |
| Acknowledged limitations | Describe unmodeled assumptions and possible impacts. |
| Acknowledged ignorance | Admit that unknown unknowns may exist. |
| Avoiding meta-ignorance | Avoid acting as if error is impossible. |

A central ethical lesson is humility: risk analysts should communicate what they know, what they do not know, and what they cannot know with current evidence.

## 11. Case Studies

### 11.1 Gambling

Gambling is used as an example of risk communication in a context where probabilities are comparatively well-defined. Different odds formats communicate the same underlying probability:

| Format | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Fractional odds | 2/1 | Win 2 units profit for every 1 unit staked. |
| Moneyline odds | +350 or -300 | Common in the United States; positive shows profit on $100, negative shows stake needed to win $100. |
| Decimal odds | 4.5 | Total return per 1 unit staked, including the original stake. |

The broader lesson is that the sophistication of a risk format depends on audience familiarity and numeracy. A format that is clear to regular bettors may be confusing to others.

### 11.2 Pharmaceuticals

Pharmaceutical risk communication must balance benefits and harms. The article discusses tools such as FDA benefit-risk frameworks, EMA effects tables, Drug Facts Boxes, and Cochrane Summary of Findings tables.

A strong practice is to present benefits and harms in the **same format**, such as expected frequencies per 1,000 people. This allows audiences to compare tradeoffs directly.

### 11.3 Climate Change

Climate change communication is challenging because it is politically contested, scientifically complex, and characterized by deep uncertainty.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change uses standardized probability language:

| Term | Probability range |
|---|---:|
| Virtually certain | 99-100% |
| Extremely likely | 95-100% |
| Very likely | 90-100% |
| Likely | 66-100% |
| More likely than not | 50-100% |
| About as likely as not | 33-66% |
| Unlikely | 0-33% |
| Very unlikely | 0-10% |
| Exceptionally unlikely | 0-1% |

Spiegelhalter notes that verbal probability labels are still interpreted inconsistently, especially across countries and languages. Pairing words with numerical ranges improves interpretation but does not eliminate ambiguity.

The IPCC also distinguishes **likelihood** from **confidence**. Likelihood concerns probability; confidence concerns evidence quality and expert agreement.

### 11.4 Toxicology and Environmental Exposures

Risk communication in toxicology is often technical and difficult for the public to interpret. Regulatory agencies use terms such as:

- Point of departure
- Reference dose
- Oral slope factor
- Uncertainty factor
- Tolerable daily intake

These are meaningful to specialists but opaque to nonspecialists. Spiegelhalter argues that agencies need better public communication, especially in controversial areas such as food additives, pesticides, acrylamide, and BPA.

### 11.5 Security and Intelligence

Security and intelligence agencies often use qualitative language such as “likely,” “highly likely,” or “severe.” However, these words can be interpreted differently.

The article discusses how intelligence agencies have struggled with whether to attach numerical probabilities to analytic judgments. Numerical estimates may be useful internally, but public communication often relies on verbal threat levels and confidence ratings.

A key issue is whether uncertainty should be aggregated into one estimate or whether decision-makers should see the range of expert disagreement.

### 11.6 Industrial Reliability

Industrial systems use methods such as Failure Mode and Effects Analysis, fault trees, event trees, and six-sigma-style measures. These are often technical and suited to professional audiences.

The article notes that technical risk notation can exclude nonspecialists. For example, scientific notation such as 4 x 10^-4 may be efficient for experts but not for general audiences.

### 11.7 Weather

Weather forecasting is a useful contrast with health risk communication. In weather forecasts, probabilities are often expressed as percentages, such as “30% chance of rain.”

A recurring problem is that audiences misunderstand the reference class. Many people think probability of precipitation refers to the fraction of the area or day affected, rather than the chance that precipitation will occur under similar forecasting conditions.

### 11.8 Natural Hazards

The article covers hurricanes, floods, earthquakes, and volcanoes.

#### Hurricanes

The National Hurricane Center’s “cone of uncertainty” is widely recognized but often misunderstood. Viewers may focus too much on the central path, even though the storm can move anywhere inside the cone.

#### Floods

The phrase “1-in-100-year flood” is often misunderstood as meaning such a flood happens only once every 100 years. A clearer expression is “1% chance per year.”

#### Earthquakes and Volcanoes

Hazard maps are common. They can be visually interpretable even when the underlying technical definition is complex.

The article notes that in low-probability, high-impact contexts, relative risk may sometimes be important. For example, even if the absolute probability of an earthquake remains low, a temporary increase in relative risk may justify precautionary action.

### 11.9 Major Industrial Accidents and F-N Curves

The article discusses F-N curves, which show the annual probability of accidents causing at least a certain number of fatalities. These curves are used to communicate societal risk rather than individual risk.

![Figure 4: F-N curve showing broadly acceptable, tolerable, and intolerable societal risk regions.](risk_uncertainty_overview_assets/figure_4_fn_curve.png)

**Key communication lesson:** F-N curves are powerful for technical and regulatory settings because they show risk over many orders of magnitude. However, they are difficult for nonspecialists and require careful explanation.

### 11.10 National Risks

Governments conduct national risk assessments for events such as pandemics, floods, power failures, industrial accidents, infectious diseases, and severe weather.

The UK National Risk Register communicates relative likelihood and impact categories. Security risks may be excluded from public numerical assessment due to classification or sensitivity.

### 11.11 Global and Existential Risks

The article discusses global catastrophic risks, such as asteroid impacts and existential threats to human survival.

These risks are difficult to communicate because they are:

- Extremely low probability
- Extremely high impact
- Hard to model from historical data
- Often based on expert judgment
- Spread across many orders of magnitude

Asteroid risk is one of the few global catastrophic risks that can be modeled statistically. The article notes that expected-value summaries may be misleading when the distribution is extremely skewed.

## 12. Main Recommendations from the Article

### 12.1 General Risk Communication Principles

Spiegelhalter’s concluding recommendations include:

- Be clear about the objective.
- Identify the audience’s needs, beliefs, and skills.
- Segment audiences rather than assuming one public.
- Develop and test communication materials with target users.
- Build trust by being trustworthy.
- Use plain language.
- Limit information to what is necessary.
- Provide layers of information: gist first, then numbers, then evidence and uncertainty.
- Admit uncertainty honestly.

### 12.2 Numerical Risk Communication Principles

Good numerical risk communication should:

- Use absolute risks where possible.
- Provide relative risks carefully and with baseline risk.
- Use percentages, proportions, or frequencies depending on context.
- Make the reference class explicit.
- Keep denominators fixed when comparing frequencies.
- Use incremental risk formats for small differences.
- Specify the time interval.
- Avoid false precision.
- Be cautious with emotionally loaded comparators.
- Use technical metrics only when the audience can understand them.

### 12.3 Visualization Principles

Good visual risk communication should:

- Use summary tables when precise comparison matters.
- Use multiple formats when the audience is diverse.
- Combine graphics with explanatory words and numbers.
- Support part-to-whole comparisons.
- Label comparators and differences clearly.
- Avoid misleading area, three-dimensional chart junk, and unnecessary decoration.
- Assume low numeracy in general-public communication.
- Avoid unnecessary animation or interactivity.
- Test visuals with actual users.

## 13. Key Terms and Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Risk | The chance and consequence of possible outcomes, often adverse outcomes. |
| Hazard | A potential source of harm. |
| Vulnerability | The susceptibility of exposed people or systems to harm. |
| Exposure | The degree to which people, assets, or systems encounter a hazard. |
| Aleatory uncertainty | Inherent randomness or unpredictability. |
| Epistemic uncertainty | Uncertainty due to limited knowledge, data, model structure, or assumptions. |
| Ontological uncertainty | Uncertainty about whether the model captures reality adequately. |
| Reference class | The group or set of cases to which a probability applies. |
| Absolute risk | The actual probability or frequency of an event. |
| Relative risk | The proportional change in risk compared with a baseline. |
| Ratio bias | The tendency for people to misinterpret risks depending on numerator and denominator. |
| Denominator neglect | Ignoring the denominator when interpreting frequencies. |
| Icon array | A visual display using icons to represent people or cases in a reference group. |
| Micromort | A 1 in 1,000,000 chance of death. |
| Funnel plot | A plot that shows estimates against sample size with statistical control limits. |
| F-N curve | A curve plotting accident fatality counts against the probability of exceeding those counts. |
| Confidence in analysis | The degree of trust warranted by the evidence, assumptions, and analytic process. |

## 14. Practical Takeaways for Data Science and Analytics

This article is highly relevant for data science because statistical analysis does not end with model output. Analysts must communicate results responsibly.

For applied analytics, the main lessons are:

1. **Do not report relative effects without baselines.** A “50% increase” may be trivial or alarming depending on the starting risk.
2. **Avoid false precision.** A model estimate of 12.347% may not be meaningfully more informative than “about 12%.”
3. **Explain who the estimate applies to.** Every probability has a reference class.
4. **Show uncertainty, but match the format to the audience.** Confidence intervals may be appropriate for technical users; qualitative caveats may be better for broad audiences.
5. **Use visuals to support comprehension, not decoration.** A simple bar chart or table may outperform a complex interactive graphic.
6. **Distinguish probability from confidence.** A high-probability statement can still rest on weak evidence, and a low-probability event can still deserve attention if consequences are severe.
7. **Test communication materials.** Expert intuition about what is clear is often wrong.

## 15. Overall Evaluation

Spiegelhalter’s article is not a simple “how-to” guide. It is a broad review showing that risk communication is context-dependent, psychologically complex, and ethically important. Its strongest contribution is integrating statistical communication with human factors: numeracy, trust, framing, affect, uncertainty, and institutional credibility.

The article’s most important message is that risk communication should be designed around the audience and objective rather than around the analyst’s preferred statistical format. Numbers, words, and graphics can all help, but each can mislead when used without context.

A concise way to summarize the article is:

> Good risk communication is not just about getting the numbers right. It is about helping the intended audience understand what the numbers mean, how uncertain they are, and how much confidence they should place in the analysis.

---

## 16. Quick Study Checklist

Use this checklist when evaluating or designing a risk communication product:

- [ ] Is the objective clear: inform, persuade, warn, support decision-making, or build trust?
- [ ] Is the intended audience defined?
- [ ] Is the reference class explicit?
- [ ] Is the time interval explicit?
- [ ] Are absolute risks shown?
- [ ] If relative risks are shown, is the baseline risk also shown?
- [ ] Are denominators fixed across comparisons?
- [ ] Are both event and non-event outcomes shown when framing could matter?
- [ ] Is uncertainty communicated without false precision?
- [ ] Are deeper limitations or model assumptions acknowledged?
- [ ] Are visuals simple enough for the audience?
- [ ] Are words like “rare,” “likely,” or “common” anchored to numbers?
- [ ] Has the communication been tested with intended users?
